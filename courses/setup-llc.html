How to Set Up an LLC

Module 1.1

What is Business Incorporation?

Chapter 1

Module 1.1: What is Business Incorporation? Business incorporation is the process of forming a legal entity, separate from its owners, that can engage in business activities, enter contracts, and provide protection against personal liability. Key Points: ● Separation: Your personal assets are protected from business debts. ● Legal Identity: Your business can enter into agreements and own property in its own name.
Module 1.2

Why Incorporate as an Artist?

Chapter 1

Module 1.2: Why Incorporate as an Artist? As an artist, incorporating your business offers several benefits: ● Professionalism: Helps you establish credibility with collaborators, venues, and clients. ● Liability Protection: Protects your personal assets from business-related lawsuits or debts. ● Tax Benefits: Offers potential tax advantages depending on your structure.
Module 1.3

LLC vs. C-Corp

Chapter 1

Module 1.3: LLC vs. C-Corp LLC (Limited Liability Company): ● Flexible ownership and management structure. ● Pass-through taxation (profits are taxed at the individual level). C-Corp: ● Separate tax entity with potential for double taxation. ● Ideal for businesses with multiple shareholders or plans to go public. Chapter 2: Benefits of Setting Up an LLC or C-Corp
Module 2.1

Limited Liability Protection

Chapter 2

Module 2.1: Limited Liability Protection Both LLCs and C-Corps provide limited liability, meaning your personal assets (home, car, savings) are protected from business debts and lawsuits.
Module 2.2

Tax Benefits and Obligations

Chapter 2

Module 2.2: Tax Benefits and Obligations ● LLCs: Income passes through to the owners, who report it on their personal tax returns. ● C-Corps: Taxed separately from the owners, which can lead to double taxation (corporate income tax + dividends).
Module 2.3

Ownership and Flexibility

Chapter 2

Module 2.3: Ownership and Flexibility LLCs offer more flexibility in management and ownership structures, while C-Corps provide opportunities for raising capital through stock issuance. Chapter 3: How to Set Up an LLC
Module 3.1

Step-by-Step Guide to Forming an LLC

Chapter 3

Module 3.1: Step-by-Step Guide to Forming an LLC 1. Choose Your Business Name: Ensure it's unique in your state. 2. File Articles of Organization: Submit to your state's Secretary of State office. 3. Create an Operating Agreement: Outlines how the LLC will be managed. 4. Obtain an E.I.N.: Needed for tax filings and bank accounts.
Module 3.2

Required Documents for LLC Formation

Chapter 3

Module 3.2: Required Documents for LLC Formation You'll need to file: ● Articles of Organization. ● Operating Agreement (optional in some states, but recommended).
Module 3.3

Filing Fees and Costs

Chapter 3

Module 3.3: Filing Fees and Costs Filing fees range from $50 to $500, depending on your state. Be prepared to pay annual fees to keep your LLC in good standing. Chapter 4: How to Set Up a C-Corporation
Module 4.1

Step-by-Step Guide to Forming a C-Corp

Chapter 4

Module 4.1: Step-by-Step Guide to Forming a C-Corp 1. Choose Your Business Name: Ensure the name is unique and complies with state requirements. 2. File Articles of Incorporation: Submit to your state's Secretary of State office. 3. Create Corporate Bylaws: These outline how the corporation will operate. 4. Appoint a Board of Directors: Required for all C-Corps. 5. Issue Stock: Distribute stock to the initial shareholders.
Module 4.2

Articles of Incorporation and Bylaws

Chapter 4

Module 4.2: Articles of Incorporation and Bylaws The Articles of Incorporation outline the structure and purpose of the corporation, while the Bylaws provide detailed rules for governance.
Module 4.3

Required Documents and Costs

Chapter 4

Module 4.3: Required Documents and Costs You'll need to submit: ● Articles of Incorporation. ● Bylaws. ● E.I.N. from the IRS. Costs range from $100 to $1,000, depending on your state and business structure. Chapter 5: Choosing Between an LLC and a C-Corp
Module 5.1

Which Structure is Right for Your Music Business?

Chapter 5

Module 5.1: Which Structure is Right for Your Music Business? Consider your long-term goals: ● LLC: Ideal for small businesses and independent artists who want flexibility. ● C-Corp: Best for artists who plan to raise capital or scale into larger operations.
Module 5.2

Tax Implications for LLCs and C-Corps

Chapter 5

Module 5.2: Tax Implications for LLCs and C-Corps LLCs: Income passes through to owners, simplifying tax filing. C-Corps: Taxed separately, with potential for double taxation (corporate and personal).

Managing Shareholders and Ownership in a C-Corp

Chapter 5

Module 5.3: Managing Shareholders and Ownership in a C-Corp C-Corps allow for multiple shareholders and the issuance of stock, making them ideal for raising investment capital. Chapter 6: Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)
Module 6.1

Do I Need a Lawyer to Incorporate?

Chapter 6

Module 6.1: Do I Need a Lawyer to Incorporate? No, you can incorporate your business yourself by following your state's procedures. However, consulting a lawyer can help if you have a more complex business structure.
Module 6.2

How Long Does Incorporation Take?

Chapter 6

Module 6.2: How Long Does Incorporation Take? Incorporation can take a few days to several weeks, depending on your state and whether you file electronically or by mail.
Module 6.3

What Happens if I Want to Switch from an LLC to a C-Corp?

Chapter 6

Module 6.3: What Happens if I Want to Switch from an LLC to a C-Corp? You can convert an LLC to a C-Corp by filing the necessary paperwork with your state, but you may face additional taxes or fees during the transition. Chapter 7: Course Conclusion and Next Steps

Recap of Key Points

Chapter 7

Module 7.1: Recap of Key Points ● LLCs: Offer flexibility and pass-through taxation. ● C-Corps: Provide opportunities for growth but come with more regulatory requirements.
Module 7.2

How to Maintain Your Business Structure and Stay Compliant

Chapter 7

Module 7.2: How to Maintain Your Business Structure and Stay Compliant ● File Annual Reports: Required for both LLCs and C-Corps. ● Pay Annual Fees: Ensure your business stays in good standing by paying any required state fees.
